From Golden Age to At-Will

The Judicial and Legislative Erosion of American Worker Protections

Union Density Decline

Private-sector union density plummeted from 35% in the mid-1950s to just 6% today, eroding just-cause protections for millions of workers.

Global Outlier

The U.S. ranks 32nd out of 38 OECD countries in labor protections, standing alone as the only developed nation without mandated paid family leave.

The United States transitioned from a mid-20th-century "golden age" of worker protections to today's near-universal "at-will" employment regime due to a confluence of factors. These include the judicial imposition of at-will employment in the late 19th century, reinforced by Lochner-era Supreme Court decisions prioritizing "freedom of contract." Subsequent legislative retreats, notably the Taft-Hartley Act of 1947, weakened unions and codified at-will employment. The exclusion of key worker groups from labor laws further fragmented worker power. In the neoliberal era, employer strategies like union avoidance and offshoring, coupled with court decisions mandating arbitration and upholding class action waivers, significantly eroded collective bargaining and legal recourse, solidifying the at-will regime.

1. The "Golden Age" of Worker Protections (1930s-1950s)

1.1 The New Deal's Labor Legislation: A Foundation for Worker Rights

The 1930s witnessed a seismic shift in American labor relations, largely driven by the economic devastation of the Great Depression and the subsequent legislative agenda of President Franklin D. Roosevelt's New Deal. A cornerstone of this era was the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA) of 1935, more commonly known as the Wagner Act. This landmark legislation fundamentally altered the balance of power between employers and employees by explicitly guaranteeing private-sector workers the right to self-organization, to form, join, or assist labor organizations, to bargain collectively through representatives of their own choosing, and to engage in concerted activities for the purpose of collective bargaining or other mutual aid and protection.

The Wagner Act was a direct response to widespread employer abuses, including spying on, interrogating, disciplining, discharging, and blacklisting union members, practices that had been rampant before its passage. To enforce these rights, the Act established the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB), an independent federal agency tasked with mediating labor disputes, guaranteeing democratic union elections, and penalizing unfair labor practices by employers.

"However, it is crucial to note that the Wagner Act's protections were not universal; it specifically excluded agricultural laborers, domestic workers, and government employees, a significant omission that disproportionately affected African American and female workers and fragmented potential worker solidarity."

Alongside the Wagner Act, other significant New Deal legislation laid the groundwork for enhanced worker protections and economic security. The Social Security Act of 1935, championed by Labor Secretary Frances Perkins, was a monumental achievement that provided a social safety net previously absent in the United States. The Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) of 1938 further solidified worker protections by establishing a federal minimum wage, mandating overtime pay for hours worked beyond 40 in a workweek for covered employees, and placing stringent restrictions on child labor.

1.2 Union Strength and Collective Bargaining Coverage

The passage of the Wagner Act in 1935 provided a powerful impetus for the growth of labor unions in the United States. Workers, emboldened by federal support, engaged in widespread organizing drives and strikes, leading to a significant increase in union density, particularly in the industrial sector. Data from the National Bureau of Economic Research shows a dramatic rise in union membership from 3.4 million in 1930 to over 14.5 million by 1947, with the most significant growth occurring in the late 1930s and during World War II.

The expansion of union membership directly translated into broader coverage by collective bargaining agreements. The "Treaty of Detroit" in 1950 between the United Auto Workers (UAW) and General Motors is a prime example of the comprehensive agreements reached during this era, providing substantial wage increases, cost-of-living adjustments, pension benefits, and health insurance, effectively establishing a model for other industries.

1.3 The Prevailing Norm of "Just-Cause" Dismissal

A critical component of the enhanced worker protections during the mid-20th-century "golden age" was the widespread establishment of the "just-cause" standard for dismissal in unionized workplaces. Unlike the "at-will" employment doctrine, which allows employers to terminate employees for any reason or no reason, the just-cause standard requires employers to have a legitimate, provable reason for disciplining or discharging an employee.

Carroll Daugherty's Seven Tests of Just Cause (1966)

  1. Notice: Was the employee forewarned of the consequences?
  2. Reasonable Rule: Was the employer's rule reasonably related to operations?
  3. Investigation: Did management investigate before disciplining?
  4. Fair Investigation: Was the investigation fair and objective?
  5. Proof: Did the investigation produce substantial evidence?
  6. Non-Discriminatory: Were rules applied fairly without discrimination?
  7. Appropriate Penalty: Was the discipline reasonably related to the offense?

The concept of just cause became a well-established principle in labor arbitration, the primary means of resolving disputes over the interpretation and application of collective bargaining agreements. These tests ensured that discipline was not only based on legitimate grounds but also administered in a procedurally fair manner, representing a significant departure from the prevailing at-will doctrine and contributing to a sense of fairness and due process for a substantial segment of the American workforce during this period.

2. The Judicial Imposition and Entrenchment of At-Will Employment

2.1 The 19th-Century Origins and Reconstruction-Era Judicial Activism

The doctrine of at-will employment, which allows employers to terminate employees for any reason or no reason at all, was not a product of democratic legislative deliberation but rather a judicial invention that emerged in the late 19th century. Legal scholars widely attribute the formal articulation of the at-will rule to Horace G. Wood's 1877 treatise, Master and Servant, where he stated that "a general or indefinite hiring is prima facie a hiring at will."

"Remarkably, subsequent analyses have shown that Wood's pronouncement was not well-supported by the case law he cited, leading many to conclude that he essentially invented the rule and presented it as established law."

The entrenchment of the at-will doctrine was significantly influenced by judicial interpretations following the passage of the Thirteenth Amendment in 1865, which abolished slavery and involuntary servitude. Proponents of the at-will rule ingeniously co-opted the Amendment's guarantee of a "right to quit" for workers, arguing that if workers possessed the fundamental right to terminate their employment at will, then a reciprocal "right to fire" at will must also exist for employers.

This argument represented a significant distortion of Congressional intent during Reconstruction, as there is no evidence that lawmakers intended the anti-subordination protections of the Thirteenth Amendment to confer upon employers an unbridled right to discharge workers without cause. This judicial activism effectively embedded a system of employer control that had racialized dimensions, particularly as employers sought new methods to manage labor, including formerly enslaved Black people and immigrant workers, in the post-slavery economy.

2.2 The Lochner Era: "Freedom of Contract" and Employer Prerogatives

The at-will employment doctrine found fertile ground for further entrenchment during the "Lochner era," a period spanning roughly from the late 19th century until the mid-1930s, characterized by the U.S. Supreme Court's vigorous application of "freedom of contract" principles to strike down labor regulations. This era takes its name from the landmark 1905 case, Lochner v. New York, in which the Supreme Court invalidated a New York state law limiting the working hours of bakers to sixty per week.

Adair v. United States (1908)

Struck down law prohibiting "yellow-dog contracts," reinforcing employer's right to fire for union activity

Coppage v. Kansas (1915)

Invalidated state law against anti-union contracts, upholding "freedom of contract" over worker rights

In Adair v. United States and Coppage v. Kansas, the Supreme Court directly reinforced the at-will employment doctrine and employer prerogatives, particularly in the context of labor organizing. These decisions constitutionalized the employer's right to fire workers for engaging in collective activity, severely hampering union organizing efforts and establishing that employers and employees bargained as equals—a notion often at odds with economic realities.

3. Legislative Retreat and the Weakening of Collective Power (1947-1980s)

3.1 The Taft-Hartley Act and the Rise of "Right-to-Work" Laws

The Labor Management Relations Act of 1947, commonly known as the Taft-Hartley Act, marked a pivotal moment in U.S. labor history, representing a significant legislative retreat from the pro-union policies of the New Deal era and a major step towards entrenching employer power. Enacted over President Harry Truman's veto, the Act was a direct response by business-friendly lawmakers to the growing strength and perceived excesses of labor unions in the immediate post-World War II period.

President Truman's Veto Message:

"This bill would conflict with important principles of our democratic society. It would go far toward weakening our trade union movement. And it contains seeds of discord which would plague this Nation for years to come."

Among its many provisions, Section 14(b) of the Taft-Hartley Act had the most profound and lasting impact on union strength by empowering states to pass "right-to-work" laws. These laws allow states to prohibit agreements between unions and employers that make union membership or the payment of union dues a condition of employment, even in workplaces where a majority of employees have voted for union representation.

Key Provisions of Taft-Hartley

  • Section 14(b): Enabled state "right-to-work" laws
  • Section 2(5): Defined employees as "at-will servants"
  • Outlawed secondary boycotts and jurisdictional strikes
  • Introduced "free speech clause" for employers
  • Required union leaders to disclaim communist affiliation

Research by Kevin Rinz analyzing the initial adoption of RTW laws following Taft-Hartley found that wage growth in highly unionized industries was 10.9% lower in the initial RTW states compared to states without such laws, demonstrating the immediate economic impact of these provisions on worker earnings.

3.2 The Decline of Union Density and Bargaining Power

The passage of the Taft-Hartley Act in 1947 marked the beginning of a sustained decline in union density and bargaining power in the United States. While union membership in the private sector peaked at around 35% of nonagricultural workers in the mid-1950s, it has since plummeted to historically low levels of just 6% today.

Union Decline Timeline

1950s: Peak union density ~35%
Early 1980s ~21%
2020s 6%

The Economic Policy Institute (EPI) highlights that the 1970s were a critical turning point, during which new unionization efforts dramatically faltered. In the 1950s and 1960s, over 1% of the employed population participated in an NLRB election each year. This figure dropped to 0.78% in the 1970s and further to 0.29% in the 1980s.

Crucial Statistic: By 1977, workers were losing more than half of NLRB representation elections, a dramatic shift from the 1940s when they won approximately 80% of these contests. Even when workers successfully voted for a union, their ability to secure a first contract declined from 86% in the 1950s to just 56% by the 1990s.

3.3 The Exclusion of Farm, Domestic, and Public Sector Workers from Key Protections

The National Labor Relations Act (NLRA) was a landmark piece of New Deal legislation enacted in 1935 with the primary purpose of establishing the legal right of most workers to organize into labor unions. However, from its inception, the statute explicitly excluded several categories of workers, including agricultural laborers, domestic workers, public employees, supervisors, and independent contractors.

Excluded Workers

  • Domestic workers
  • Public employees
  • Supervisors
  • Independent contractors

Impact

  • Fragmented worker solidarity
  • Created two-tiered labor system

The exclusion of agricultural and domestic workers from the NLRA was a critical decision that left a substantial portion of the American workforce without federal protections for organizing and collective bargaining. At the time of the Act's passage, these sectors employed a significant number of African American workers, particularly in the South.

The consequences of these exclusions were far-reaching. By omitting large segments of the workforce, the Wagner Act effectively fragmented the labor movement from its inception. It created a two-tiered system of labor rights, where industrial workers gained significant protections and bargaining power, while agricultural and domestic workers were largely left to fend for themselves. This division weakened the overall political and economic power of the labor movement, as a united front including these sectors could have been a more formidable force.

3.4 The Failure to Enact Federal Unjust Dismissal Legislation

The period following the "golden age" of labor saw a significant decline in union density and bargaining power in the United States. As union protections waned, the default rule of at-will employment became increasingly dominant. In this context, the absence of a federal unjust dismissal statute, or "just cause" protection for all workers, became a glaring gap in U.S. labor law.

Missed Legislative Opportunities

  • 1978 Labor Law Reform Act: Failed to overcome Senate filibuster despite considerable support
  • 1993-94 Striker Replacement Bills: Failed to pass legislation prohibiting permanent replacement of striking workers
  • PRO Act (2020-21): Passed House but repeatedly stalled in Senate

Efforts to enact federal legislation providing broader protection against unjust dismissal have been made over the years, but none have succeeded in becoming law. These initiatives have consistently faced strong opposition from business groups who argue that such laws would impose undue burdens on employers, stifle economic growth, and interfere with managerial prerogatives. The lack of a federal unjust dismissal statute has left workers in most states reliant on a patchwork of common law exceptions to the at-will rule, which are often narrowly interpreted and difficult to enforce.

4. Neoliberal Shifts and Court-Driven Erosion of Rights (1970s-2000s)

4.1 Employer Strategies: Union Avoidance, Offshoring, and Fissured Work

The latter decades of the 20th century witnessed a significant transformation in the American labor landscape, characterized by a pronounced shift towards neoliberal economic policies and aggressive employer strategies aimed at circumventing unionization and reducing labor costs. Employers increasingly adopted sophisticated "union avoidance" consulting services, which specialized in tactics to dissuade workers from organizing or to defeat union certification drives.

Union Avoidance

Captive audience meetings, one-on-one supervisor meetings, anti-union propaganda

Offshoring

Relocating production to regions with lower labor costs and weaker protections

Fissured Work

Subcontracting, franchising, temporary staffing to shed employment responsibilities

Another critical development was the rise of "fissured work" arrangements, where companies increasingly relied on subcontracting, franchising, and the use of temporary staffing agencies to perform work previously done by direct employees. This strategy allowed lead companies to shed direct employment responsibilities, including providing benefits, adhering to labor standards, and facing potential unionization drives. Workers in these fissured arrangements often faced precarious employment, lower wages, and a lack of clear lines of accountability.

4.2 Supreme Court Decisions: Mandatory Arbitration and Class Action Waivers

The period from the 1970s through the 2000s witnessed a significant judicial shift that further eroded workers' abilities to challenge unfair labor practices and employment conditions, particularly through the expansion of mandatory arbitration agreements and the enforcement of class action waivers. Key Supreme Court decisions played a pivotal role in this transformation, effectively closing the courthouse doors to many workers.

Landmark Supreme Court Cases

Gilmer v. Interstate/Johnson Lane Corp. (1991)

Held that employees could be compelled to arbitrate age discrimination claims based on employment arbitration agreements

Circuit City Stores, Inc. v. Adams (2001)

Reaffirmed that the Federal Arbitration Act applied to most employment contracts

Epic Systems Corp. v. Lewis (2018)

Upheld arbitration agreements requiring individual arbitration and prohibiting class actions

In Epic Systems Corp. v. Lewis, the Supreme Court, in a 5-4 decision, held that arbitration agreements requiring employees to arbitrate their claims individually and prohibiting class or collective actions are enforceable under the Federal Arbitration Act. This decision was a major victory for employers and a significant blow to workers' ability to band together to challenge systemic workplace issues, such as widespread wage theft or discrimination.

4.3 The Proliferation of Mandatory Arbitration Clauses and Non-Compete Agreements

The Supreme Court's endorsement of mandatory arbitration in employment contracts has led to a dramatic proliferation of such clauses in the American workplace. Employers, emboldened by judicial backing, increasingly require employees to sign arbitration agreements as a condition of employment, often burying these clauses in lengthy employment contracts that workers may not fully understand.

Alongside the rise of mandatory arbitration, there has been a significant increase in the use of non-compete agreements, even for low-wage workers and in industries where trade secrets are not a primary concern. The Economic Policy Institute (EPI) notes that the presentation of a non-compete agreement can be seen as a proposal to renegotiate the at-will relationship, often with little to no additional consideration provided to the employee.

Recent Development: The Federal Trade Commission has recently proposed a rule to ban most non-compete agreements, citing research that they negatively affect labor markets and reduce wages by nearly $300 billion annually.

5. The Current Landscape of U.S. Employment Law (2020s)

5.1 The Prevalence of At-Will Employment vs. Just-Cause Protections

In the 2020s, the at-will employment doctrine remains the default rule governing the vast majority of employment relationships in the United States. This means that, absent a specific contractual provision or statutory protection to the contrary, employers are generally free to terminate employees for any reason—good, bad, or nonexistent—as long as the reason is not illegal.

Current At-Will Employment Coverage

While some states recognize limited common law exceptions to at-will employment (e.g., public policy exceptions, implied contract exceptions based on employee handbooks), these are often narrowly construed by courts and provide far less comprehensive protection than a true "just cause" standard. The continued prevalence of at-will employment means that most American workers lack robust job security and can be dismissed without the employer needing to demonstrate a legitimate, performance-related reason or follow fair procedures.

5.2 International Comparison: The U.S. as an Outlier in Worker Protections

The United States stands in stark contrast to most other developed nations, particularly its peers in the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD), regarding the legal framework governing employment termination. While many OECD countries mandate that employers must demonstrate "just cause" for dismissing an employee, the U.S. largely adheres to the doctrine of "at-will" employment.

This contrasts with the U.S. approach, where federal law provides minimal protection against unjust dismissal for most private-sector employees.

6. Explanatory Narrative: Factors Driving the Transition to At-Will Employment

6.1 Internal Divisions within the Labor Movement

The decline of union strength and the corresponding rise of at-will employment in the United States cannot be fully understood without examining the internal divisions that have historically plagued the labor movement. The exclusion of public sector workers from the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA) meant that their path to unionization and collective bargaining rights developed separately and often later than that of private sector workers.

Structural Divisions in U.S. Labor Movement

  • Public vs. Private Sector: Different legal frameworks and organizing challenges
  • Occupational Exclusions: Agricultural and domestic workers left unprotected
  • Geographic Variation: State-by-state differences in labor laws
  • Political Fragmentation: Divergent agendas and priorities

The historical exclusion of agricultural and domestic workers from the NLRA also represents a profound internal division. By denying these predominantly Black and, later, Latino and immigrant workforces the basic right to collective bargaining, the New Deal labor framework effectively created a two-tiered system of labor rights. A movement divided along lines of industry, sector, race, or immigration status is inherently less able to mount a cohesive defense of worker rights or to advocate effectively for policies that would benefit all workers.

6.2 The Influence of Southern Segregationist Politics and Anti-Communism

The trajectory of U.S. labor law and the entrenchment of at-will employment were significantly shaped by the powerful influence of Southern segregationist politics and the pervasive anti-communism of the mid-20th century. Southern Democrats, who held considerable sway in Congress due to seniority and the one-party "Solid South," played a crucial role in shaping New Deal labor legislation, most notably by ensuring the exclusion of agricultural and domestic workers from the protections of the Wagner Act.

"These exclusions were not accidental; they were a deliberate strategy to maintain a low-wage, racially stratified labor system in the South, where a significant portion of African American workers were employed in these sectors."

The post-World War II era saw the rise of virulent anti-communism, which was skillfully wielded as a weapon against the labor movement. The Taft-Hartley Act of 1947 included provisions requiring union leaders to file affidavits disclaiming affiliation with the Communist Party. This was a clear attempt to purge left-wing influence from unions and to tarnish the image of organized labor as un-American. The combination of Southern segregationist opposition to empowering Black workers and the national anti-communist crusade created powerful headwinds against the labor movement.

6.3 Business Mobilization and the Rise of Employer-Side Legal Advocacy

The erosion of worker protections and the ascendance of at-will employment were significantly propelled by a concerted and well-funded mobilization by the business community, particularly from the 1970s onwards. A pivotal moment in this mobilization was the 1971 "Powell Memo" (officially titled "Attack on American Free Enterprise System"), written by future Supreme Court Justice Lewis F. Powell Jr. for the U.S. Chamber of Commerce.

Key Elements of Business Mobilization

  • Legal Advocacy: Development of employer-side labor law expertise
  • Union Avoidance: Sophisticated consulting services to prevent organizing
  • Political Lobbying: Business associations influencing legislation
  • Alternative Dispute Resolution: Promoting mandatory arbitration over court litigation

A key component of this business mobilization was the strategic development and deployment of employer-side legal advocacy. Law firms specializing in labor and employment law began to offer sophisticated "union avoidance" consulting services, helping companies to prevent union organizing drives or to decertify existing unions. The success of this legal strategy is evident in Supreme Court decisions like Gilmer and Epic Systems, which upheld the enforceability of mandatory arbitration clauses and class action waivers.

6.4 The Enduring Impact of "Freedom of Contract" Judicial Doctrine

The judicial doctrine of "freedom of contract," which gained prominence during the Lochner era (roughly 1897-1937), has had an enduring and detrimental impact on efforts to establish robust worker protections and to move away from the at-will employment regime in the United States. This doctrine often presumes a mythical equality of bargaining power between individual workers and corporate employers, ignoring the stark economic realities that give employers a dominant position in the employment relationship.

Impact on Modern Employment Law

  • Elevates contractual liberty over worker security concerns
  • Presumes equal bargaining power between employers and individual employees
  • Used to challenge legislative attempts to mandate employment terms
  • Continues to influence judicial deference to employer-friendly arbitration agreements

The Supreme Court's decisions in cases like Adair v. United States (1908) and Coppage v. Kansas (1915) during the Lochner era directly invoked freedom of contract to strike down laws protecting workers' rights to organize. Although these specific precedents were later eroded by New Deal legislation, the broader ideological underpinnings of freedom of contract have proven resilient. The Court's more recent jurisprudence favoring mandatory arbitration agreements reflects a deference to contractual arrangements even when they are imposed on employees on a "take it or leave it" basis with little real negotiation.

6.5 Missed Legislative Opportunities to Strengthen Worker Protections

Throughout the latter half of the 20th century and into the 21st, several critical legislative opportunities to strengthen worker protections and potentially curb the excesses of the at-will employment regime were missed, contributing to the current landscape of diminished job security.

Failed Labor Law Reform Act (1978)

Aimed to expedite union elections and strengthen remedies against employer interference, but failed to overcome Senate filibuster

Striker Replacement Bills (1993-94)

Proposed banning permanent replacement of striking workers, but faced strong business opposition

PRO Act (2020-21)

Comprehensive labor law reform passed House but repeatedly stalled in Senate

Unjust Dismissal Legislation

Multiple attempts to establish federal "just cause" standard all failed

Each of these missed legislative chances represents a fork in the road where the U.S. could have chosen a path towards greater worker empowerment and job security, but instead, the status quo of employer dominance and at-will employment was reinforced. The cumulative effect of these failures has been a continued erosion of worker protections and a legal framework that lags behind international standards.

7. Policy Recommendations: Rebalancing Labor Relations and Restoring Worker Protections

7.1 Federal Just-Cause Legislation and Strengthening the NLRA

To address the fundamental imbalance in U.S. labor relations and provide meaningful job security, Congress should enact federal legislation establishing a "just cause" standard for all employee dismissals. This would replace the default at-will employment rule, requiring employers to demonstrate a legitimate, job-related reason (such as poor performance, misconduct, or genuine economic necessity) for terminating an employee, and to follow fair and transparent procedures.

Key Components of Just-Cause Legislation

  • Clear definition of "just cause" drawing from established labor arbitration principles
  • Burden of proof on employer to demonstrate legitimate reason for dismissal
  • Requirement for fair and transparent procedures
  • Robust enforcement mechanisms and remedies (reinstatement, back pay)
  • Protection against retaliation for exercising rights

Concurrently, the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA) must be significantly strengthened to protect workers' rights to organize and bargain collectively. This includes imposing stricter penalties on employers who engage in unfair labor practices (such as firing workers for union activity), streamlining the union certification process, facilitating first-contract negotiations, and prohibiting the permanent replacement of striking workers. These reforms would help rebuild worker power and ensure that collective bargaining can effectively establish fair terms and conditions of employment.

7.2 Reforming Arbitration Practices and Banning Coercive Non-Compete Agreements

The widespread use of mandatory arbitration clauses and class action waivers has severely undermined workers' access to justice. Legislation is needed to reform arbitration practices in employment disputes. This should include banning pre-dispute mandatory arbitration agreements for statutory employment claims (such as discrimination, harassment, and wage theft) and prohibiting class action waivers.

Arbitration Reform

  • Ban pre-dispute mandatory arbitration
  • Prohibit class action waivers
  • Ensure true voluntariness if used
  • Require transparency and due process
  • Provide for meaningful judicial review

Non-Compete Reform

  • Federal ban on coercive non-competes
  • Protection for low-wage workers
  • Narrow tailoring requirements
  • Adequate consideration standards
  • Enforcement of FTC proposed rule

Furthermore, coercive non-compete agreements, particularly those imposed on low-wage and middle-wage workers who lack access to trade secrets or specialized client relationships, should be banned at the federal level. The Federal Trade Commission's (FTC) proposed rule to ban most non-competes is a step in the right direction and should be finalized and robustly enforced.

7.3 Expanding Coverage and Protections for Excluded Worker Groups

The historical exclusions of agricultural workers, domestic workers, and certain public sector employees from key labor protections have created significant vulnerabilities and inequities in the U.S. labor market. Federal labor laws, including the NLRA and the FLSA, must be amended to extend full coverage and protections to these historically marginalized groups.

Coverage Expansion Priorities

  • Agricultural Workers: Full NLRA and FLSA coverage, right to organize and overtime pay
  • Domestic Workers: Equal protection under labor laws and collective bargaining rights
  • Public Sector: Federal baseline standards for collective bargaining rights
  • Misclassified Workers: Stronger enforcement against employee misclassification
  • Gig Economy: Clear definitions ensuring platform workers receive full protections

This means ensuring that farmworkers and domestic workers have the unequivocal right to organize, bargain collectively, receive minimum wage and overtime pay, and benefit from health and safety protections on par with other workers. For public sector employees, federal legislation should establish baseline standards for collective bargaining rights and ensure that all public servants, including those in states with restrictive laws, can exercise their fundamental labor rights.

7.4 Drawing Lessons from International Labor Standards

The United States lags significantly behind other developed nations in terms of worker protections and job security. Policymakers should actively study and draw lessons from international labor standards and the practices of countries with stronger worker rights. This includes examining the "just cause" dismissal regimes prevalent in most OECD countries, which place the burden on employers to justify terminations and provide robust procedural safeguards.

European Models

  • Germany: Social justification requirements
  • France: Strict dismissal procedures
  • UK: Fair reason and fair process
  • Sweden: "Last in, first out" principles

Additional Protections

  • Mandated paid family leave
  • Sectoral bargaining systems
  • Stronger unemployment benefits
  • Worker representation on boards

The U.S. should also consider adopting policies like mandated paid family and sick leave, which are standard in nearly all other OECD nations but absent at the federal level in the U.S. Furthermore, exploring models of sectoral bargaining, where wages and working conditions are negotiated across entire industries rather than just at the enterprise level, could help raise standards for all workers and reduce income inequality. International conventions of the International Labour Organization (ILO) provide important benchmarks that the U.S. should strive to meet.
